7 Common Causes of Freezer Not Freezing (But Fridge Works)

In Toronto homes, this specific problem accounts for 25% of all refrigerator service calls. The good news is that most causes are repairable and don't require replacing the entire unit.

1. Evaporator Fan Motor Failure

Frequency: 35% of cases

Symptoms: Freezer gradually warms up over 24-48 hours. You don't hear the fan running when the door is closed. Fridge stays cool because it's closer to the cooling source.

Why it happens: The evaporator fan circulates cold air from the evaporator coils throughout both compartments. When it fails, cold air doesn't reach the freezer properly. Samsung and LG models from 2015-2020 have higher fan motor failure rates.

Solution: Replace evaporator fan motor. Professional repair recommended ($180-$250).

2. Defrost System Malfunction

Frequency: 30% of cases

Symptoms: Heavy frost buildup on evaporator coils (behind back panel). Freezer temperature slowly rises. You may hear the compressor running constantly.

Why it happens: The defrost heater, defrost timer, or defrost thermostat fails. Ice accumulates on evaporator coils, blocking airflow. Common in Whirlpool and Maytag models.

Solution: Replace defrost system component ($120-$280 depending on part).

3. Blocked Air Vents

Frequency: 20% of cases

Symptoms: Some areas of freezer are colder than others. Fridge works perfectly. Airflow seems restricted when you hold hand near vents.

Why it happens: Food packages block air vents between fridge and freezer. Ice buildup covers vents. Overstuffing prevents air circulation. Very common in Toronto where people stock up during cold winters.

Solution: Clear vents and reorganize contents (DIY, free). Success rate: 65%.

4. Damaged Door Seal (Gasket)

Frequency: 8% of cases

Symptoms: Frost around door edges. Freezer door feels warm on outside. Higher electricity bills. You can see light or feel air when door is closed.

Why it happens: Rubber gasket cracks over time (especially in dry Toronto winters). Gasket becomes dirty and doesn't seal. Door hinges misaligned.

Solution: Clean or replace door gasket ($80-$150). DIY cleaning success rate: 40%.

5. Temperature Control Thermostat Issue

Frequency: 5% of cases

Symptoms: Freezer temperature fluctuates wildly. Sometimes it freezes, sometimes it doesn't. Temperature dial doesn't seem to work.

Why it happens: The thermostat that controls cooling cycles fails. It can't properly signal the compressor when to run. GE and Frigidaire models have known thermostat issues.

Solution: Replace temperature control thermostat ($120-$200).

6. Low Refrigerant (Sealed System Leak)

Frequency: 1-2% of cases

Symptoms: Both fridge and freezer gradually become warmer over weeks. You might hear hissing sounds. Oil spots visible near compressor.

Why it happens: Very rare. Sealed system develops a leak. Refrigerant escapes slowly. This is an expensive repair.

Solution: Sealed system repair or replacement ($400-$600). Often better to replace unit.

7. Compressor Problems

Frequency: 1% of freezer-specific cases

Symptoms: Compressor runs but isn't pumping refrigerant efficiently. Both compartments affected but freezer fails first since it needs colder temperature.

Why it happens: Compressor wearing out (usually 10+ years old). Start relay failure.

Solution: Compressor replacement ($400-$600) or replace unit if over 10 years old.

5 DIY Troubleshooting Steps to Fix Your Freezer

Step 1: Check and Clear Air Vents

Why: Blocked vents are the easiest fix with highest success rate.

How to do it:

  1. Remove all items from freezer and fridge
  2. Locate air vents (usually on back wall of freezer, or between compartments)
  3. Check for ice buildup covering vents
  4. Check for food packages pushed against vents
  5. Clear at least 3 inches of space around all vents
  6. Use a vacuum to remove dust from vent openings
  7. Reorganize freezer with proper spacing

Cost: $0 (DIY), 20 minutes

Success rate: Fixes 65% of airflow-related issues

Safety warning: Don't use sharp objects to chip ice - you can puncture refrigerant lines.

Step 2: Test the Evaporator Fan

Why: Fan motor failure is the #1 cause. Testing takes 2 minutes.

How to do it:

  1. Open freezer door and listen for fan noise (fan stops when door opens on most models)
  2. Press door switch (small button on door frame) with door open
  3. Fan should start running when switch is pressed
  4. Fan should be quiet and smooth (no grinding or squealing)
  5. Locate fan behind back panel inside freezer (if accessible)
  6. Check if fan blades spin freely by hand (unplug first!)

Cost: $0 (DIY test), 5 minutes

Success rate: Identifies problem 90% of time if fan is faulty

Safety warning: Unplug refrigerator before touching any internal components.

Step 3: Manual Defrost to Remove Ice Buildup

Why: Ice on evaporator coils blocks airflow. Defrosting can restore cooling temporarily.

How to do it:

  1. Remove all food (use coolers with ice packs)
  2. Unplug refrigerator completely
  3. Open both freezer and fridge doors
  4. Place towels on floor to catch water
  5. Let defrost naturally for 8-12 hours (never use heat gun - fire hazard)
  6. Speed up with fans blowing room-temperature air
  7. Wipe all surfaces dry
  8. Plug back in and wait 4-6 hours before restocking

Cost: $0 (DIY), 8-12 hours

Success rate: Fixes 50% of defrost-related issues temporarily (if defrost system is broken, problem returns in weeks)

Step 4: Clean and Inspect Door Gasket

Why: Dirty or damaged seals let warm air in, preventing proper freezing.

How to do it:

  1. Mix warm water with mild dish soap
  2. Clean entire door gasket with soft cloth
  3. Remove any food residue or mold
  4. Dry thoroughly
  5. Check for cracks, tears, or hard spots
  6. Do the dollar bill test: close door on dollar bill, pull gently - should have resistance
  7. Repeat test at multiple spots around door

Cost: $0 (cleaning), 15 minutes

Success rate: Fixes 40% of minor seal issues

Note: If gasket is damaged, replacement costs $80-$150 professionally.

Step 5: Adjust Temperature Settings

Why: Sometimes settings get accidentally changed or thermostat needs adjustment.

How to do it:

  1. Locate temperature control dial or digital display
  2. Set freezer to 0¬∞F (-18¬∞C) - the coldest safe setting
  3. Set refrigerator to 37¬∞F (3¬∞C)
  4. Wait 24 hours for temperature to stabilize
  5. Place freezer thermometer between frozen items
  6. Check temperature after 24 hours
  7. Adjust dial if needed (colder setting if above 10¬∞F)

Cost: $0-$15 (freezer thermometer), 5 minutes + 24 hour wait

Success rate: Fixes 20% of settings-related issues

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is my freezer not freezing but the fridge is cold?

When your freezer isn't freezing but the fridge works, it usually indicates a problem with the evaporator fan (35% of cases), defrost system malfunction (30%), or blocked air vents (20%). The refrigerator section receives cold air first, so freezer-specific issues affect only the freezer compartment. Check if the evaporator fan is running when you open the freezer door - it should stop when opened. If you don't hear any fan noise when door is closed, the fan motor likely failed. Most Toronto homes experience this issue during winter when people overstock freezers, blocking air vents.

Can a freezer stop working while the fridge still works?

Yes, a freezer can stop working independently because refrigerators use a single cooling system with air circulation controlled by fans and dampers. The evaporator coils are typically in the freezer, and cold air is circulated to the fridge. If the evaporator fan fails, air vent gets blocked, or the defrost system malfunctions, the freezer won't freeze while the residual cold air keeps the fridge somewhat cool. This is actually a common problem - about 25% of refrigerator repair calls in Toronto involve a freezer that won't freeze while the fridge works. Samsung and LG french-door models (2015-2020) are particularly prone to this issue.
